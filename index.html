Introduction to HCI and Interaction Design

1. What Is Human–Computer Interaction (HCI)?

Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) is a cross-disciplinary field that studies how people interact with computers. The "I" in HCI refers to both interaction (the abstract model) and interface (the physical/software tools).

HCI focuses on the design, implementation, and evaluation of interactive systems. It combines knowledge from:

  • Engineering
  • Psychology
  • Ergonomics
  • Design

2. Why HCI Is Important

Computers are used in almost all areas of life. Good HCI ensures:

  • Easy use
  • Efficiency
  • Safety
  • Correct task completion
Key: Good usability leads to higher productivity.

3. Interaction vs. Interface

ConceptDefinition
InteractionThe abstract model of how users perform a task with a computer
InterfaceThe physical or software tools that implement the interaction (e.g., mouse, keyboard, screen)

4. Usability in HCI

High usability means systems are:

  • Easy to learn
  • Efficient to use
  • Safe
  • Help users complete tasks correctly

5. User Experience (UX)

UX includes more than usability. It covers:

  • Functionality
  • Usability
  • Aesthetic design
  • Emotional satisfaction
  • Integration into daily life
Key: Good UX can make users prefer a product even if similar products exist. (Example: iPod's success was due to quality UX — simple, elegant, pleasurable.)

6. Why Good HCI Design Is Difficult

HCI design must consider many factors simultaneously:

  • Different user types
  • Task characteristics
  • Device capabilities and cost
  • Lack of exact usability measurements
  • Rapidly changing technology

Designers need knowledge from many different fields.

7. The Six HCI Principles

Principle 1: "Know Thy User" (User-Centered Design)

Interfaces should be designed around target users, not developers. Design must match users' needs, abilities, and skills.

Principle 2: Understand the Task

Design should be based on a clear understanding of the task — the job a user wants to complete. Designers must identify the sequence of steps and subtask structure.

Principle 3: Reduce Memory Load

Interfaces should require as little memory as possible. Short-term memory (STM) holds only 5–9 items (Miller's "magic number").

Principle 4: Strive for Consistency

Consistency reduces memory load and helps users learn faster. Types: within an application and across applications. Inconsistency causes confusion and more errors.

Principle 5: Prevent Errors and Allow Reversal

Show only relevant options; disable invalid items; prefer selection over typing. Choosing is safer than remembering and typing.

Principle 6: Naturalness

Interfaces should feel natural and familiar. Includes:

  • Metaphorical design — use real-life metaphors (e.g., virtual sphere for rotation)
  • Affordance — the design shows how it should be used; makes interaction intuitive and self-explanatory
  • Natural interaction — gestures, natural language, everyday actions

8. Interaction Design (ID)

"Designing interactive products to support the way people communicate and interact in their everyday and working lives." — Sharp, Rogers, and Preece (2019)

ID is multidisciplinary. Designers must understand how people think, behave, communicate, and interact — plus emotions, aesthetics, business, and technology.

Four Basic Activities of Interaction Design

  1. Discovering requirements
  2. Designing alternatives
  3. Prototyping alternative designs
  4. Evaluating the product and its UX throughout

Core Characteristics of Interaction Design

  • Users involved throughout development
  • Specific usability and UX goals identified and documented early
  • Iteration through core activities

9. HCI vs. Interaction Design

AspectHCIInteraction Design
FocusStudying how people use computersDesigning how people interact with products
ScopeOften focuses on computer systemsIncludes physical and digital products

Fundamental Theories in HCI

1. Why Theories Matter in HCI

Effective HCI design requires understanding both computer factors (software/hardware) and human behavior (how users think, perceive, and act). Technology alone does NOT guarantee good usability.

Key: Poor understanding of users leads to poor interaction design. Human abilities and limitations directly affect interaction quality.

2. What Are HCI Theories?

HCI theories are scientific explanations of how humans perceive, think, and act when interacting with computer systems. They help designers understand user behavior and design interfaces that match human abilities and limitations.

3. Theoretical Foundation 1: Cognitive Science

Focuses on mental processes involved in interaction: problem solving, attention, memory, decision making, task modeling, and GOMS models.

Human Problem-Solving Model

Three main parts:

  1. Sensation & Perception — receive and interpret external input (visual, audio, touch)
  2. Memory — short-term information + long-term knowledge
  3. Decision Maker & Executor — creates plans, chooses actions based on memory and perception

Task Modelling

Helps designers understand steps needed to achieve a system goal. Structure: Goal → Sub-goal → Action → Object.

Example (Online Shopping): Goal = Buy a product → Sub-goal = Add to cart → Action = Tap "Add to Cart" → Object = Product item

GOMS Model (Goals, Operators, Methods, Selection Rules)

A cognitive model to analyze task performance by breaking tasks into:

  • Goals — what the user wants to achieve
  • Operators — actions like clicking, typing, thinking
  • Methods — sequences of steps
  • Selection rules — choosing between methods

Example (Save a file): Method 1: File → Save. Method 2: Ctrl+S. Selection Rule: experienced → shortcut; beginner → menu.

4. Theoretical Foundation 2: Human Factors

Focuses on sensory and physical capabilities of users.

Visual Perception

How users see and interpret visual information. Design implications: use high contrast, readable fonts, avoid cluttered layouts.

Auditory Perception

How users hear and understand sound feedback. Design: use simple alert sounds; do not rely only on sound for important messages.

Tactile Perception

How users feel physical feedback (e.g., vibration). Important for mobile and wearable devices.

Ergonomics and Motor Control

How users physically move and control devices. Design: place important buttons within easy reach, avoid small clickable targets. Example: large buttons at bottom of mobile screen (thumb zone).

Fitts's Law

MT = a + b × log₂(A / W + 1)

Where: MT = Movement Time, A = Distance to target, W = Target width.

  • Larger targets → easier to select
  • Closer targets → faster to reach
  • Important buttons should be big and near the user
Concept Link: Fitts's Law directly supports L3 (Design Guidelines) — e.g., minimum touch target size (9mm). Cognitive Science concepts (memory, attention) connect to L4 (Human Capabilities & Constraints) and inform L7 (Prototyping) decisions about layout and flow.

HCI Design Principles, Guidelines & Usability Testing

1. What Are HCI Guidelines?

HCI Guidelines are specific, practical recommendations that tell designers how to design and implement an interface to improve usability and user experience.

Key characteristics:

  • Specific and detailed (not abstract)
  • Context-dependent (depend on users, tasks, platform, environment)
  • Derived from HCI principles, theories, and practical experience

2. Principles vs. Guidelines vs. Theories

LevelDescriptionExample
TheoriesScientific explanations (abstract)Fitts's Law, Cognitive Science
PrinciplesGeneral rules that guide design thinking"Know Thy User", Consistency
GuidelinesSpecific, practical recommendations"Touch targets ≥ 9mm", "Use high contrast"

3. Why Guidelines Are Important

Real systems are complex and constrained. Guidelines:

  • Reduce design errors
  • Promote consistency
  • Support evidence-based design
  • Especially useful for novice designers

4. Categories of HCI Design Guidelines

a) Visual Display Layout

  • Avoid clutter and overload
  • Visually prioritize important information
  • Proper alignment, spacing, and limited colors reduce cognitive load

b) Information Structuring

  • Organize by user goals and tasks
  • Group related content logically
  • Clear hierarchies help understanding

c) Navigation Design

  • Users should always know where they are and where they can go
  • No dead ends
  • Simple navigation reduces mental effort and frustration

d) Taking User Input

  • Minimize typing and manual data entry
  • Prefer selection over recall
  • Use default values and auto-completion
  • Keep input formats consistent
  • Clear labeling prevents mistakes

e) Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG-based)

PrincipleMeaning
PerceivableText alternatives, sufficient contrast
OperableKeyboard access, enough time
UnderstandablePredictable behavior, readable text
RobustCompatible with assistive technologies
Key: Accessibility is both ethical and legal.

5. Limitations of Guidelines

Guidelines have limitations: they can be too specific, may conflict with each other, and cannot cover every situation. Designers must use judgment.

6. Usability Testing

High usability leads to higher productivity, fewer errors, and greater user satisfaction. Usability is a foundation of UX.

Usability Evaluation Criteria (What We Measure)

  • Learnability — how easy for new users
  • Efficiency — speed of task completion
  • Errors — frequency and severity
  • Satisfaction — user contentment

Usability Evaluation Methods (How We Measure)

  • Observation study — watch users perform tasks
  • Measurement-Based Testing — quantitative: task completion time, error rate, success rate
  • Surveys / Questionnaires — measure satisfaction
Concept Link: Guidelines here build on L1's principles and L2's theories (e.g., Fitts's Law → minimum touch target size). WCAG accessibility guidelines connect to L9 (Accessibility & Inclusivity). Usability testing methods are expanded in L8.

Human Capabilities and Constraints

1. Why Study the Human Side in HCI?

When something goes wrong, the first reaction is often to blame the user. HCI takes a different position: users are normal human beings with natural limitations. Most interaction problems are caused by designs that ignore these limitations, not by technical failures.

2. Core Assumption

Key: Human limitations are normal, expected, and shared by all users to different degrees. Systems that assume unlimited memory, constant attention, perfect perception, or precise motor control will inevitably cause interaction problems.

3. Humans and Computers Are Different

ComputersHumans
Process information consistently and preciselyForget, lose attention, misinterpret, vary in ability

Interaction occurs at the boundary between these two different systems. HCI studies the consequences of this difference.

4. Human Information Processing

Interaction involves three stages: Perception → Cognition → Action. Each stage introduces potential limitations that may cause interaction difficulties if ignored.

5. Memory Constraints

Human working memory is limited. Users forget steps, previous choices, or system rules — especially across multiple screens. Relying on recall rather than recognition increases cognitive load and leads to errors.

6. Attention Constraints

Human attention is limited and easily disrupted. Users may miss updates, overlook feedback, or lose context. Designs that rely on sustained attention often fail in real-world contexts.

7. Perceptual Constraints

Perception is influenced by context and expectations. Icons may be misinterpreted; important information may be overlooked. The gap between intended meaning and perceived meaning is a common source of problems.

8. Motor Control Constraints

Motor abilities vary across users and contexts. Accidental actions, difficulty with small targets, and imprecise movements are common. Designs assuming high precision increase errors.

9. Variability Between Users

There is no single "average" user. Users differ in cognitive, sensory, and physical abilities, plus experience and context. HCI designs for ranges of capabilities, not idealized users.

10. From Limitations to Interaction Problems

Reasoning Pattern: Human limitation → Interaction difficulty → Usability problem

This chain of reasoning is fundamental to HCI analysis.

11. Key Takeaways

  • Human limitations are normal
  • Interaction problems often result from ignored constraints
  • Understanding users is the foundation of effective HCI

User-Centered Design Process

1. What Is User-Centered Design (UCD)?

User-Centered Design places the user at the center of the design process. Instead of starting with technology or features, UCD begins with understanding users, their tasks, and their context. The goal: build a system that works for the user.

2. Why UCD Matters

Systems often fail not because they are technically incorrect, but because they do not align with user needs. UCD helps:

  • Reduce usability problems
  • Improve user satisfaction
  • Align system behavior with human capabilities
Key: "A design that is not based on user understanding is likely to fail, regardless of how technically correct it is."

3. Core Principle of UCD

Users should be involved throughout the design process — not limited to a single stage. This includes observing users, gathering feedback, and evaluating designs with them.

4. The UCD Process (Conceptual Cycle)

A repeating cycle of four key activities:

  1. Identifying user needs and requirements
  2. Exploring alternative design solutions
  3. Developing prototypes
  4. Evaluating designs with users
Key: "Design evolves through iteration, not through a single correct solution." "Prototypes are not final solutions, but tools for exploring and refining ideas."

5. Why Alternatives Matter

Considering multiple design alternatives prevents premature decisions, encourages exploration, and supports stronger design reasoning. A single early idea is rarely the best.

6. Understanding Users, Tasks, and Context

ElementWhat to Understand
UsersWho they are, what they want to achieve, what challenges they face
TasksGoals, actions
ContextPhysical environment, device constraints, social factors, time pressure

7. Evaluation in UCD

Evaluation must be continuous. It provides feedback that drives the next design iteration and helps identify interaction problems.

8. Key Takeaways

  • Design should start with users
  • Design is iterative and cyclical
  • Multiple design alternatives are essential
  • Prototypes support exploration and refinement
  • Evaluation drives improvement
  • Design balances user needs and system constraints
Concept Link: UCD is the practical application of L1's "Know Thy User" and L4's constraints. The UCD cycle directly feeds into L6 (User Research) for requirements gathering, L7 (Prototyping) for exploring alternatives, and L8 (Usability Testing) for evaluation. Context understanding here connects to L9 (Real-World Contexts).

User Research

1. What Is User Research?

User research refers to methods used to understand users, their behavior, needs, and interaction with systems. It includes Data Gathering — systematically collecting information about users. Its purpose is to inform and justify design decisions.

2. Why User Research Matters

Designing without research leads to incorrect assumptions, misaligned features, and poor usability. Design decisions should be based on data, not intuition or guesses.

3. Common Data Gathering Techniques

TechniqueCharacteristicsCaptures
InterviewsFlexible, in-depth, qualitativeWhat users say
QuestionnairesScalable, quantifiable, limited depthBroad patterns
ObservationCaptures real behavior, reveals hidden issues, time-consumingWhat users actually do
Field StudiesReal context, rich data, less controlReal-world behavior
Key "Say" vs. "Do": Interviews capture what users say; Observation captures what users actually do. Users may not accurately describe their behavior.

4. Task Analysis

Focuses on understanding user goals, steps, and workflows. Includes breaking tasks into structured steps and analyzing relationships.

5. Personas

Personas are fictional representations of users based on real data. They capture goals, behaviors, skills, and context. Personas should be based on real user data, not assumptions.

6. Types of Data

TypeExplains
QualitativeWhy behavior occurs
QuantitativeHow often behavior occurs

7. Planning User Research

Includes: defining goals, selecting techniques, identifying participants, and defining clear tasks.

8. Participants and Sampling

Select representative users. A small number of carefully selected participants can provide valuable insights.

9. Combining Techniques (Triangulation)

Using multiple techniques improves reliability and reduces bias.

10. Data Collection Settings

SettingAdvantage
Field (real-world)Realism
Lab (controlled)Precision

11. From Data to Insight

Raw data must be organized, interpreted, and analyzed to reveal meaningful patterns that inform design.

12. User Research vs. Usability Testing

  • User research = understanding users (before/during design)
  • Usability testing = evaluating designs (during/after design)
Concept Link: User research is the "Identifying needs" stage of the L5 UCD cycle. Techniques like interviews and observation inform the creation of L7 Prototypes. Personas connect to L4's principle that there is no average user. Triangulation improves the reliability of data used in L8 (Usability Testing).

Prototyping

1. What Is a Prototype?

A prototype is an early and simplified version of a system used to explore, test, and communicate design ideas. It is NOT the final product — it's a thinking and testing tool.

2. Why Prototyping Matters

  • Detect usability problems early
  • Explore alternative solutions
  • Reduce development cost and risk
  • Improve communication between designers and stakeholders
Key: "Prototyping reduces risk by testing ideas before full implementation." "Prototyping allows designers to fail early and learn from mistakes."

3. Prototyping in the Design Process

Central part of iterative design: understand users → create prototype → evaluate → refine. Iteration may lead to minor improvements or complete redesign.

4. Prototypes as Thinking Tools

Not only for presenting ideas, but for thinking through design problems. Early prototypes are often messy and incomplete — this is expected.

5. Prototype Dimensions

DimensionDescribes
FidelityHow detailed the prototype is
TypeWhy we are building it
StructureHow much of the system we cover

6. Fidelity in Prototyping

LevelDescriptionWhen Used
Low-FidelitySimple, rough: sketches, paper prototypes, storyboards. Fast, flexible, easy to modify.Early stages to explore ideas
High-FidelityDetailed, realistic, interactive, visually refined. Closer to final system.Later stages for testing
Warning: High-fidelity prototypes may give users the impression the system is complete, which can affect feedback quality.

Storyboards

Represent how interaction unfolds over time. Useful for understanding user journeys and scenarios.

7. Types of Prototypes by Purpose

TypePurpose
ExploratoryGenerate ideas and explore alternatives (early stage)
ExperimentalTest specific concepts or design decisions
EvolutionaryRefined over time; may evolve into the final system
ThrowawayCreated quickly and discarded after use

8. Horizontal vs. Vertical Prototypes

TypeCoveragePurpose
HorizontalMany features, shallow interactionExploring system structure
VerticalOne feature, deep interactionTesting specific functionality

9. Choosing the Right Prototype

Depends on: stage, goal, scope, and constraints (time, cost, tools). Early → low-fidelity; later → high-fidelity.

10. Trade-offs in Prototyping

Low-fi: fast, flexible but less realistic. High-fi: realistic but time-consuming. Designers must balance speed vs. realism.

11. Common Mistakes

  • Treating prototypes as final designs
  • Adding too much detail too early
  • Skipping low-fidelity stages
  • Ignoring feedback
  • Failing to iterate
Concept Link: Prototyping is the third activity in L5's UCD cycle. Prototypes should address L4's human constraints (e.g., testing whether navigation overloads working memory). They are based on insights from L6 (User Research) and are evaluated using methods from L8 (Usability Testing). Low-fidelity prototypes help test L3's guidelines before committing to code.

Usability Principles and Testing

1. Usability and System Failure

Repeated user errors are indicators of design limitations, not user failure. A system may work correctly technically yet fail to support users in practice.

Key: When multiple users encounter the same difficulty, the system should be examined before blaming users.

2. Defining Usability

Usability = the extent to which a system enables users to achieve goals effectively, efficiently, and with satisfaction.

These three dimensions are distinct: a system can be effective but inefficient, or efficient but unsatisfying.

Usability = Effectiveness + Efficiency + Satisfaction
Key: Successful task completion alone does not guarantee usability.

3. User Behavior and Expectations

Users rely on learned patterns of attention and mental models from prior experience. Users look where they expect information — not where it actually exists.

4. Usability Testing

Observe users performing realistic tasks. Minimize evaluator interference to allow authentic behavior to emerge.

5. Characteristics of User Behavior

Users rely on heuristics, act under time pressure, and make decisions based on incomplete information. Fast interaction ≠ correct decisions.

6. Evidence-Based Evaluation

Evaluation should be grounded in observable behavior rather than subjective opinion. Observable behavior enables actionable design decisions.

7. Identifying Underlying Problems

Visible issues are often symptoms; underlying causes shape user errors. Root-cause analysis is essential.

8. Design Decisions and Trade-offs

Adding features may increase functionality but also complexity. Effective design prioritizes clarity.

Key: Solve the real problem, not the surface issue.

9. Heuristic Principles

General usability principles that guide design. Visibility is fundamental — if users can't perceive key information, they can't make effective decisions.

10. Measuring Usability

Metrics: success rate, time on task, errors, number of clicks.

Key: Metrics describe what; observation explains why. Metrics are mainly quantitative data.

11. System-Level Perspective

Early-stage failures propagate and affect outcomes downstream. Usability emerges from interactions between system structure and user behavior.

12. AI and Decision Support

Poor usability → poor data → poor AI decisions. AI relies on user actions, not user intentions.

13. Evaluating Design Improvements

A/B testing compares user behavior before and after changes. Small design adjustments often produce significant improvements.

14. Limitations of Usability Testing

Usability testing is not one-time — it's an ongoing, iterative process. Systems, users, and contexts change over time.

15. Trade-offs in Design

All design decisions involve trade-offs between simplicity and functionality.

16. Core Principle

Key: A system succeeds when it aligns with user mental models. Errors occur due to poor support for decision-making, not missing functionality.

17. Synthesis

Heuristics guide design, testing validates it, and AI scales it. Systems should primarily adapt to users, but users may adapt in cases of safety, technical constraints, or standardization.

Concept Link: This lecture synthesizes many earlier concepts: L4's constraints explain why usability problems occur; L3's guidelines provide how to prevent them. A/B testing connects to L6's quantitative methods. The AI–usability connection here foreshadows L10 (AI Interaction) and L11 (Trust & Ethics). Mental models connect to L1's naturalness principle.

Human-Centered Systems in Real-World Contexts

Core Idea

Usability depends on how well a system adapts to different users and the conditions in which it is used. A system cannot be evaluated in isolation — it must be evaluated in relation to who is using it and under what conditions.

Three-Layer Structure

LayerFocus
Layer 1: UsersWho is using the system
Layer 2: ContextUnder what conditions
Layer 3: System ResponseHow the system reacts

Layer 1: Users

Diversity of Users

Users differ in abilities, experience, goals, and expectations. Systems must support multiple levels of expertise.

Accessibility and Inclusivity

Accessibility ensures users can interact; Inclusivity extends this to consider different backgrounds, experience levels, and expectations.

  • Small text → increased reading difficulty
  • Complex layouts → increased confusion
Key Insight: Accessibility → Lower cognitive load → Better decisions

Layer 2: Context

Context of Use

Conditions: device, environment, and user attention. Desktop users may compare carefully; mobile users make quick decisions due to distraction.

Key Insight: Context → Behavior → Decisions

Mobile and Context Constraints

Mobile introduces: limited screen space, reduced attention, time pressure. Design must support fast and low-effort decision-making.

Layer 3: System Response

Feedback in Context

Feedback answers: What happened? What is happening? What will happen next? Without feedback, users repeat actions → errors.

Key Insight: Feedback → Reduces uncertainty → Increases confidence

Errors and Recovery

Errors are more likely when cognitive load increases. Prevent errors and support recovery (warnings before critical actions, easy correction).

Key Insight: High cognitive load → More errors → Need recovery support

Integration

User differences + Context conditions → Shape behavior → Influence decisions → Determine outcomes

The system may remain unchanged, but results vary depending on who uses it and under what conditions.

Core Concepts Summary

  • User differences affect interpretation
  • Context changes behavior
  • Feedback reduces uncertainty
  • Errors increase under pressure
Concept Link: User diversity connects to L4's "no average user." Context of use is a key element in L5's UCD framework. Feedback and error recovery tie to L3's usability guidelines and L8's heuristic principles (visibility, error prevention). Accessibility connects to L3's WCAG principles. Mobile constraints inform L10 (Context-Aware AI).

Designing AI Interaction

Purpose

How AI changes interaction design. AI systems learn from users, make predictions, and influence decisions over time. The goal: understand how to design AI systems correctly.

Case Study: AI Hiring System

A company uses AI to recommend candidates based on past hiring data. The system prefers candidates from specific regions because of historical patterns, even though education has improved everywhere.

Key: The system is not intentionally unfair — it learns from past data and reproduces patterns. The system appears correct, but its decisions may no longer reflect reality.

Core Model of AI Interaction

A continuous cycle:

  1. Data (past behavior)
  2. Model (learned patterns)
  3. Output (recommendations)
  4. User Response (new behavior → becomes new data)

Every design decision affects this cycle.

Topic 1: AI-Driven Interfaces (Output Stage)

AI suggests options before users act — the system is no longer neutral. Outputs define what users can consider.

  • Design rule: Support decisions, don't replace them
  • Boundary: Don't fully automate when human judgment is important or outcomes have significant impact
  • Consequence: Users lose awareness; important options disappear

Topic 2: Personalization (Model Stage)

Personalization = filtering and prioritizing based on learned patterns. It improves efficiency but:

  • Reduces diversity — favors similar results
  • Limits exploration — harder to discover new options
Design rule: Personalization must include exploration. Avoid strong personalization when diversity or comparison is needed.

Topic 3: User Interaction with AI (Response Stage)

Users accept, reject, or modify outputs. Risk: users may trust the system more than necessary.

  • Allow override
  • Provide multiple options
  • Enable correction
Critical Insight: The system may become "confidently wrong" — producing strong but incorrect outputs.

Topic 4: Context and Change (Data Stage)

Real-world conditions change. AI learns the past, not the present. Past data may not reflect current reality.

  • Update data regularly
  • Monitor real-world changes
  • Adapt system behavior
  • Simplify interaction for mobile

Topic 5: Emerging Interaction Paradigms

Interaction changes from navigation to communication (e.g., chatbots). But new interfaces don't fix underlying model problems.

Interface TypeBest For
ConversationalFlexible, exploratory tasks
StructuredPrecise tasks requiring comparison
Key Insight: AI systems do not only support decisions — they shape user behavior and future outcomes. They may reinforce outdated patterns, limit awareness, or produce confident but incorrect results.
Concept Link: The AI interaction cycle is an extension of L8's observation that poor usability → poor AI. Personalization connects to L4's perceptual constraints (users see only what's shown). Context and change build on L9's real-world context layer. User control requirements connect to L1's "Know Thy User" and L5's UCD principle. These topics lead directly into L11 (Trust, Ethics & Error).

Trust, Ethics, and Error in AI Interaction Systems

Purpose

How trust, ethics, and error influence AI interaction systems. Building on L10's AI cycle, these factors affect the entire Data → Model → Output → User cycle.

Core Idea: AI systems must be designed to be trustworthy, fair, and reliable. They influence decisions and shape user behavior over time.

1. Mental Models and Trust (User Stage)

Users form expectations based on prior experience (mental models). The system follows its own model based on data/algorithms. When these don't align → mismatch.

Consequences of mismatch:

  • Confusion
  • Reduced trust
  • Incorrect decisions
  • Users may trust the system even when wrong
Design rule: Support calibrated trust, not blind trust. Make system behavior understandable. Show how decisions are made.

2. Context, Attention, and Fairness (Data Stage)

Human attention is limited. In different contexts (mobile), users process information differently. This can lead to:

  • Biased decisions
  • Reduced fairness
  • Over-reliance on system outputs
Key: Fairness is not only a model property — it's also about how users interact with the system. Don't assume users have full attention.

3. Errors and Uncertainty (Output Stage)

AI can produce confident but incorrect results. Users may not be aware of uncertainty.

  • Indicate confidence levels
  • Show alternative options
  • Allow users to question outputs
Design rule: Do NOT present uncertain results as certain. Avoid hiding uncertainty or presenting outputs as absolute truths.

4. Human–AI Interaction and Control (Response Stage)

Users may accept system outputs without questioning. Over-automation reduces control.

  • Allow override
  • Provide multiple options
  • Enable correction and feedback
Design rule: Users must remain in control of decisions. Avoid full automation in high-impact decisions.

5. Designing for Trustworthy AI (System-Level)

Trust must be designed, not assumed. Design should support:

  • Transparency — explain system outputs
  • Clarity — clear feedback
  • User control — reduce ambiguity
  • Error prevention

6. Final Insights

Key: AI systems are not just tools. They shape what users see, think, and choose. Designing AI is not only about system performance — it's about supporting human understanding, fairness, trust, and responsible decision-making.

Key Risks:

  • Reinforce outdated patterns
  • Limit awareness — users may not realize options are hidden
  • Produce confident but incorrect results

Structured vs. Conversational (Revisited)

Structured interfaces (tables, filters, comparisons) support clearer, more consistent, and more confident decisions than conversational interfaces for evaluation tasks.
